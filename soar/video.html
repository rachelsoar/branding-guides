SOAR Video Brand Guide

Visual direction for all Soar-branded video content. When in doubt, reference this page before making production decisions.

Visual Identity

The Soar Look: Light, Open, Human

Soar's visual identity is built on one idea: technology that serves people should look like it belongs in their world, not in a sci-fi movie. Think open sky, warm light, natural texture. The aesthetic is expansive and grounded — never cold, never aggressive, never "tech for tech's sake."

Living Green

Icons & Imagery

What to Use, Where to Find It

Icons

Google Material Symbols Outlined — this is the only icon library for Soar. Clean, minimal line icons. For social media brand icons (LinkedIn, X, Instagram, YouTube), use Font Awesome. Never use custom 3D icons, glowing orbs, or illustrated icon sets.

Stock Footage & Photos

Source from Pexels, Unsplash, or Canva's stock library for modern footage. For historical or humanity-focused segments, archival footage works beautifully — black-and-white history, old film grain, real moments from real eras. Archive.org is a strong source for public domain material. Always verify licensing before using anything: confirm it's public domain, Creative Commons, or royalty-free. If there's any doubt about rights, don't use it — find an alternative. No AI-generated stock imagery. No composites of floating elements. If it looks like a sci-fi movie poster, it's wrong.

Product Screenshots

Pull directly from the live sites. Show them clean — in a browser frame or device mockup on a solid or gradient background. Screenshots are proof the product is real. Don't add effects that make them look fake.

Creative Direction

What Soar Looks Like on Screen

Every visual choice should answer one question: does this make the viewer feel like this company cares about people, or about looking impressive?

Use This

✓
Cloud and sky imagery — dawn, golden hour, open horizon. The visual metaphor for "soar" is literal: air, light, lift.
✓
Real humans in real settings. Candid over posed. Genuine expressions over stock smiles.
✓
Warm, natural color grading. Slight warmth in shadows, clean highlights. Think documentary, not commercial.
✓
Clean cuts and gentle dissolves. Let the content breathe. Transitions should be invisible.
✓
Clean gradient backgrounds — soft whites to off-whites, light coral-to-cream, subtle sky tones. Minimalist and uncluttered. Let color do the work, not texture.
✓
Breathing room. Generous white space in graphics. Generous pauses in edit. Don't fill every second.
✓
Product screenshots shown clean — in a browser frame or device mockup. No effects layered on top.

Never This

✗
Glitch effects, digital distortion, data-stream overlays, or matrix-style text. This is not a cyberpunk brand.
✗
Neon or electric color accents. No acid green, hot pink, or electric blue outside the brand palette.
✗
"AI brain" stock footage — neural networks, glowing circuits, robot faces, binary code rain. We are not that company.
✗
Tech-bro transitions — whip pans with motion blur, zoom punches, RGB split, lens distortion effects.
✗
Dark backgrounds as the dominant visual. Soar is light. Navy sections are accent, not the baseline.
✗
3D renders, heavy particle effects, floating holographic UI, or sci-fi HUD overlays. Subtle warm glow and bloom are fine (see Transitions & Effects) — but neon glow, electric outlines, and cold blue/teal light effects are off-brand. If motion graphics are needed, use minimal vector line animations — clean, flat, 2D. Think thin lines drawing themselves on, simple icon reveals, subtle scale shifts. Not 3D spinning globes or tech dashboards.
✗
Default or robotic-sounding AI voices. If using AI narration, the voice must sound natural, warm, and conversational — not polished-announcer, not monotone, not obviously synthetic. Test it: if a viewer would notice it's AI, it's wrong. Slow the pacing, vary the cadence, choose a voice with natural breath and warmth.
Production Rules

Motion, Sound & Pacing

Transitions & Effects You Can Use

Cross dissolves for emotional beats. Light leaks and warm light washes between sections — these give energy without being techy. Slow push-ins or pull-outs (Ken Burns) on stills and archival footage. Luma fades (fade through white instead of black — keeps it light). Soft whip pans for pace. Subtle warm glow and bloom on highlights is welcome — keep it soft and golden, not neon. Gentle lens flare in warm tones. Light film grain on archival segments. These are your creative tools. Use them.

Lower Thirds

Urbanist font. Left-aligned. Subtle fade-in, hold, fade-out. Use a coral accent line or pill to tie it to the brand. Keep it minimal — name, title, gone. Don't overthink the animation. It should appear, do its job, and leave without competing with the speaker.

Music

Warm, organic, understated. Acoustic instruments over synths. Think "NPR feature" not "product launch hype reel." Music supports the voice — it never competes with it.

Pacing

Breathe. A 3-minute video with room to think beats an 8-minute video that rushes. Let important lines land. Silence after a strong statement is more powerful than filling every gap.

Text on Screen

Urbanist only. Sentence case (not ALL CAPS unless it's a 2-word label). White or navy on backgrounds. Coral for emphasis or accent words. Keep supers to 6 words max.

Voice

AI narration must sound indistinguishable from a real person. Warm, natural cadence — not a default text-to-speech voice. Adjust speed, tone, and breathing to feel human. When possible, let Paul's own clips carry the story — his real voice is the strongest asset. The AI voice fills gaps, it doesn't replace him.

Showing UI & Websites

Product screenshots and website footage should fill the screen or sit inside a clean device mockup (laptop, phone frame). No floating windows over dark or techy backgrounds. No distortion, tilt, or perspective warp on the screenshot. No glowing borders or neon backdrops behind the browser. The product is the visual — present it clean, well-lit, and at full resolution. If the UI is well-designed, it doesn't need decoration. Let it speak.

People & Photography

Never AI-edit anyone's face or appearance. No smoothing, no wrinkle removal, no enhancement. Use real photos and actual video stills exactly as they are. Never composite a person onto a fake background with floating elements behind them. If introducing someone on screen, use their real footage — a clean still or video clip with a simple lower third. The person is the content, not the backdrop.

Title Cards & Intros

Name + title in Urbanist on a clean background (solid color, subtle gradient, or a single relevant photo). No 3D text, no beveled or embossed lettering, no drop shadows on type, no glow effects. No busy composites with multiple background elements (buildings, DNA, network diagrams, floating icons). One background. One name. One title. Done.

Multi-Brand Note

When a Video Features Sub-Brands

Soar corporate is the parent brand. When showing Citizen Portal, Workplace AI, or Fleet of Agents within a Soar video, use each product's own colors only within their segment — then return to Soar corporate palette. Don't blend palettes across the whole video. Each product gets its moment, then we come home to coral and navy.
