Gradients

When and How to Use Gradients

Gradients are a signature part of the WAI look. The website uses them heavily — hero backgrounds, section transitions, card accents. Social posts should follow the same rules. A gradient done right feels premium. A gradient done wrong feels like a PowerPoint from 2009.

Lavender → White
Light card backgrounds, hero sections. The default "light mode" gradient.
Violet → Blue
CTA buttons, accent bars, headline backgrounds. The signature WAI gradient.
Near-Black → Deep Violet
Dark mode backgrounds, premium sections. White text on top.

Gradient rules

Only use gradients between colors in the WAI palette — violet, blue, lavender, near-black, white. Two colors max per gradient. Direction: 135° diagonal or 180° vertical. Never horizontal — it reads as a loading bar. Gradients are backgrounds, not foregrounds — text and UI elements sit on top of them, they never compete.

When to use them

Hero graphics, section backgrounds, CTA buttons, and card accents. Don't gradient everything — a post with a clean white background and a single violet headline is often stronger than a gradient card. Use gradients when you want the post to feel premium or when you need to distinguish a section. A solid color is always a safe default.

Headlines: Urbanist — weights 700–800
Body: Outfit — weights 400–600
No other fonts. Ever.
Voice & Tone

How Workplace AI Sounds

WAI's voice is the sharp colleague who simplifies everything. Not the corporate handbook. Not the excitable startup. The person in the room who says the clear thing everyone was thinking.

High Focus Language — The Core Rule

All WAI copy follows the High Focus framework: communication centered on the reader, not on us. High Focus means "you/your/yours" dominates the copy. Low Focus means "we/our/us/I" dominates. Every sentence should pass the test: is this about what they get, or about what we built?

Convert it or kill it. If a phrase is Low Focus, either flip it to High Focus or delete it entirely. There is no third option.

Dessert first. The benefit to the reader goes at the top of the post, not buried at the end. People scroll past before reaching the meat — put the outcome upfront.

The reader is the hero. They improve. They win. They discover. Shift subjects and verbs so the reader is the doer, not the observer of what you built.

Conversion examples
Low Focus (about us)
"We use AI to automatically extract action items from your meetings"
High Focus (about them)
"Your action items show up in your inbox before you close the meeting tab"
Low Focus
"Our platform provides searchable meeting transcripts"
High Focus
"Find any decision from any meeting — search it like email"
Low Focus
"We help companies improve their meeting culture"
High Focus
"Your team gets 40% of their calendar back when meetings actually lead somewhere"

Outcome over feature

Don't say what the product does. Say what the person gets. "Never lose an action item again" beats "AI-powered action item extraction." Lead with the result, not the mechanism.

Confident, not loud

No exclamation marks in headlines. No "game-changing" or "revolutionary." State the value plainly and let the reader decide it's impressive. Understatement earns more trust than hype.

Specific over generic

"Your Monday stand-up, summarized before you finish your coffee" beats "AI meeting summaries for teams." Name the real scenario. Use numbers when you have them.

Short sentences win

Social copy gets scanned, not read. One idea per sentence. One sentence per line. If a post needs a second paragraph, the first paragraph is too long.

We — only for behind-the-build

In behind-the-scenes and team posts, talk as "we" — a team that built something. "We built this because nobody should have to take notes and pay attention at the same time." But for feature posts, tips, and value copy, stay in High Focus: "you/your" language only. "We" is for stories about the team. "You" is for everything else.

Meetings are the pain point

Everyone hates bad meetings. That's the universal entry point. Start there and lead to the solution. Don't start with AI — start with the problem AI solves.

Content Categories

What to Post

Every post should fit one of these. If it doesn't, it probably doesn't belong on the feed. Aim for 3–5 posts per week.

Feature spotlight — one feature, one benefit, one visual. 1–2×/week.
Meeting tip — practical advice, doesn't always mention the product. 1×/week.
Product update — what changed and why it matters. When there's real news.
Social proof — testimonials, user quotes, usage stats. 1× every 2 weeks.
Behind the build — team, decisions, what we're working on. 1× every 2 weeks.
Engagement hook — questions, polls, hot takes about meetings. 1×/week.

Creative Direction

What WAI Posts Look Like

Billboard test: if someone scrolled past in 2 seconds, would they know what it's about and who it's from?

Use This

✓
Clean white or lavender backgrounds with violet accents. Let the whitespace do the work.
✓
One headline per graphic. Six words max.
✓
Real product screenshots in clean device mockups. The UI is the proof.
✓
Stats and numbers as the hero. "40% of meetings could be async" on a clean card beats any stock photo.
✓
Dark mode variants (near-black bg, white text, violet accents) sparingly — 1 in 4 posts max.
✓
WAI logo mark on every graphic — small, corner placement.

Never This

✗
Stock photos of people in suits pointing at whiteboards or shaking hands.
✗
Busy backgrounds, gradient meshes, circuit boards, "digital transformation" imagery.
✗
Colors outside the palette. If it's not in the swatch section, it's not in the post.
✗
AI brain imagery — neural networks, robot faces, glowing circuits, binary code.
✗
Exclamation marks, ALL CAPS body text, or fire/rocket emojis.
✗
Canva templates that look like Canva templates. No blobs, starbursts, or wavy dividers.
